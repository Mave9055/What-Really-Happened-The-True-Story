Deployment Ready – VA Pilot Edition

What Really Happened

A 12‑Session Trauma Psychoeducation Program

Facilitator Manual – Kali Reaper (Daniel Lingar)

1. Facilitator Core Rules

🎯 Performance Rules (in the room)

  • PAUSE – Count to 3 silently after any reframe or key line.
  • DROP – Lower your voice on the second half of an important sentence.
  • BREATHE – Take one slow breath before starting the rapid reframes.
  • EYES – Look at one person (not the whole room) when asking a mirror question.

🛡️ Safety Rules (Plan B)

  • Yellow Light (participant mildly activated): Pause, lead micro‑regulation (feet on floor, one breath).
  • Red Light (participant flooded): They can leave without asking. No explanation needed.
  • Room feels heavy (your call): Stop teaching. Say: “Let’s take 30 seconds – feet on floor, slow breath.”
  • You (facilitator) get flooded: Say “I need a moment.” Pause, take three breaths. Model regulation.

⛔ Hard limit: No more than 2 sessions back‑to‑back. Period.

🧘 After every session – decompression (5 min):
“Write down one thing you noticed about the room – not to fix, just to see. Then shake out your hands, drink water, and leave the session there. You are not responsible for their healing. You are responsible for the container.”

2. Session 00: The Anchor (Intake – 70 min)

Mandatory pre‑requisite. Do not skip.

Facilitator script – read verbatim

Opening (0:00 – 5:00)

(Walk in. Settle. Make eye contact with a few people. Let there be 5 seconds of silence before you speak.)

"Welcome. Before we begin any content, we need to build the container. Nothing in this program will require you to relive your trauma. You are here to understand how trauma works – not to perform your pain."

(Pause. Let that land.)

"My name is [your name], and I'll be your facilitator for this curriculum. Over the next 12 sessions – plus this intake today – we're going to study the mechanisms of Complex PTSD through one documented life. A man named Daniel."

"But first, we need to make sure this room feels safe enough for that to happen. So we're going to start with a short grounding exercise. This isn't about relaxing you. It's about orienting you – helping your nervous system know: I'm here. I'm not there anymore."

3‑2‑1 Grounding (5:00 – 10:00)

(Lead slowly. Pause after each instruction.)

"Silently identify 3 blue objects in this room."

(Wait 10 seconds.)

"Now notice 2 different textures. Maybe the chair you're sitting in. Maybe the fabric of your sleeve. Just notice."

(Wait 10 seconds.)

"Listen for 1 sound outside this room. Not inside – outside. A car, a bird, the wind. Just one sound."

(Wait 10 seconds.)

"Feel the weight of your body in the chair. Notice that the floor is solid beneath your feet."

(Pause.)

"You are here, in this room, on [today's date]."

(Pause.)

"Take a breath in… and as you exhale, blow out slowly through pursed lips – like you're blowing through a straw. Make the exhale longer than the inhale."

(Lead one breath with them. Then wait.)

"Good. That's a tool you can use anytime you feel unsteady – here or in life."

Define the Frame (10:00 – 20:00)

"Let me be clear about what this program is – and what it is not."

"This is psychoeducation. That means we are here to study how trauma works – the mechanisms, the biology, the patterns. We are not here to process our personal trauma stories. We are not a therapy group. We do not require anyone to share their history."

(Pause.)

"We learn by looking at one life – Daniel's. His story becomes our case study. Why? Because when you look at someone else's life first, you can see the patterns clearly – without immediately getting pulled into your own. That distance gives you clarity."

"You will never be asked to relive or describe your own trauma in this room. If something comes up for you, you use your Plan B tools – which I'm about to give you."

Distribute Handouts (20:00 – 30:00)

(Hand out the Plan B card and the Confidentiality & Working Agreement to each person.)

"Take a moment to look at these. The blue card is your Plan B. It's what you do when the material gets heavy. The white page is our working agreement – the rules that keep this room safe."

"Let me walk you through the Plan B first."

Plan B Protocol – Yellow & Red Light (30:00 – 45:00)

"At some point in this program, your nervous system is going to react. You might feel anxious, angry, numb, or disconnected. That is not a problem. That is your system doing exactly what it was trained to do."

"We don't ignore it. We don't push through it. We work with it."

"Yellow Light" – if you notice early signs of activation – maybe your heart speeds up, your mind goes blank, your chest feels tight – that's a yellow light. You stay in your seat, and you regulate. You can press your heels into the floor. You can name five blue things in the room. You can take a slow breath out like we just did. You're bringing your system back online.

"Red Light" – if you feel flooded – overwhelmed, like you're going to cry or scream or shut down completely – that's a red light. You do not push through. You leave the room. You do not need to ask permission. You do not need to explain. Just quietly step out. Go to the designated quiet space – or the restroom. Splash cold water on your face. Walk briskly for a minute. Then come back when you're ready. You will never be trapped in this room.

"Facilitator pause" – if I notice the room getting heavy – if I see several people looking frozen, or the energy drops, or someone leaves – I will stop teaching. I will say: "Let's take 30 seconds – feet on the floor, slow breath." We will ground together. Because we prioritize the people in the room over the material.

"If I get flooded" – facilitators are human too. If I need a moment, I'll say: "I need a moment." I'll pause, take three breaths, and model regulation. That's not failure. That's showing you what it looks like to stay safe.

"Any questions about Plan B?"

(Answer questions briefly. Don't over‑explain.)

Confidentiality & Working Agreement (45:00 – 55:00)

"Now turn to the white page – our Confidentiality & Working Agreement. I'm going to read through it, then we'll all sign."

"What's said in this room stays in this room. What's learned here can leave with you."

"No crosstalk." When someone speaks, we listen without interrupting, without trying to fix them. We don't give advice unless it's asked for.

"Right to pass." You never have to speak. Silence is valid participation.

"Focus on mechanism, not graphic details." We are here to understand how trauma works – not to share the most painful moments of our lives. That keeps everyone safe.

"Digital boundaries." No recording, no photographing, no posting about sessions on social media – not even vaguely. This space only works if people feel protected in it.

"Facilitator is a mandated reporter." That means if I believe you are at risk of harming yourself or someone else, or if you report abuse of a child, elder, or vulnerable adult, I am legally required to report it. I want you to know that now, so there are no surprises.

"If you agree to these terms, sign and date at the bottom. Then pass the page to me."

(Collect signatures. Give people time.)

Closing Grounding (55:00 – 65:00)

"We're almost done for today. Before we close, we're going to ground again – this time to help you transition back to the rest of your day."

"Wiggle your toes. Stretch your arms. Press your palms together. Feel the boundary of your own skin."

(Pause.)

"You are leaving this room as yourself – not as a case study, not as a patient. Just as a person who came to understand something."

"You are here. You are present. And you are safe."

(Pause.)

"Take one last slow breath out."

Final Logistics & Next Session (65:00 – 70:00)

"Our first content session – Session 01 – will be [day and time]. We'll meet in this same room. It will be 30‑35 minutes. Please bring your Plan B card with you – keep it somewhere you'll see it."

"Between now and then, nothing is required of you. But if you notice your nervous system reacting – maybe feeling more on edge, or more numb – that's normal. You're not doing anything wrong. Just notice it."

"Any final questions?"

(Answer briefly.)

"Thank you for being here. You've already done the hardest part – you showed up. See you at Session 01."

(End. No filler.)

4. Session 01 – The Blueprint: Survival Over Connection

Cold open: “Before you were born, your body was already taking notes. Daniel’s did. Thirty days in a glass box – no skin, no heartbeat, just wires and absence. That wasn’t just a medical event. That was his first lesson: connection is dangerous. Distance is safe. And he spent forty years proving it right. Sound familiar? Then stay.”

Core concept (excerpt): VLBW birth, NICU trauma, attachment wounding, freeze response during domestic violence, CPTSD before age ten.

Mirror question: “Think of one time you were judged for freezing, for shutting down, for not ‘doing something.’ What if that wasn’t a failure – but a survival response?”

Rapid reframes:
Lazy → exhausted nervous system
Coward → freeze response
Broken → adapted to an unlivable situation
Too sensitive → a nervous system that learned to scream because whispering never worked

Open loop behavior: freezing or pulling back from someone

Cliffhanger: “So that’s the blueprint – distance, danger, hypervigilance. But here’s what Daniel didn’t know for forty years: the blueprint can be redrawn. Next session: the masks of perfection and people‑pleasing.”

5. Session 02 – The Masks We Wear: Fawning & Perfectionism

Cold open: “Daniel never said no. He covered shifts with a migraine. He made everyone laugh while his chest was caving in. The world called him a hard worker. Trauma science calls it the fawn response – panic dressed up as reliability.”

Mirror: “Think about the last time you said ‘yes’ when you meant ‘no.’ What were you afraid would happen?”

People‑pleasing → appeasement reflex
Workaholic → using productivity to feel safe
Lazy → burnout from chronic overfunctioning
Selfish → the first boundary you ever allowed yourself

Open loop behavior: saying yes when you mean no

6. Session 03 – When Love Hurts: Trauma Bonds & Love Bombing

Cold open: “Daniel didn’t chase toxic women because he lacked self‑respect. He chased them because they felt like home – and home had always been chaos.”

Obsession → anxious attachment
“Soulmate” → trauma bond
“Finally someone sees me” → love bombing
“I’m too much” → you were finally being honest

Open loop behavior: feeling addicted to someone’s attention

7. Session 04 – The War on Reality: Gaslighting & Blame Shifting

Cold open: “Gaslighting doesn’t break you with yelling. It breaks you with whispers: ‘That never happened. You’re too sensitive.’”

Crazy → gaslit
Forgetful → trauma amnesia
Overreacting → responding to invisible threats
The problem → the scapegoat

Open loop behavior: doubting your own memory or perception

8. Session 05 – Relapse Isn’t a Betrayal: It’s a Scream

Cold open: “Daniel didn’t choose meth over his kids. He chose meth because the pain was unbearable and no one taught him another way to make it stop.”

Relapse → feedback, not failure
Addict → someone who needs better tools
Weak → exhausted from fighting alone
Manipulative → desperate to be heard

Open loop behavior: wanting to escape or numb

9. Session 06 – What Healing Actually Looks Like

Cold open: “Healing is not a sunrise. It’s not a moment when everything clicks. For Daniel, healing started with a pen – writing rage letters and burning them.”

Falling apart → finally feeling
Needing help → being human
Slow progress → real progress
Not healed yet → still fighting

Open loop behavior: avoiding rest or feeling guilty for stopping

10. Session 07 – Grief, Guilt & Breaking Point

Cold open: “June 24, 2011. Daniel’s brother Garrett died in a car wreck. The guilt nearly swallowed him. ‘If I’d woken him up. If I’d let him come.’ That’s survivor’s guilt – and it’s a lie.”

Crying → courage
Guilt → false responsibility
Falling apart → telling the truth
Not over it → still honoring them

Open loop behavior: replaying guilt about a loss

11. Session 08 – Shame vs. Responsibility

Cold open: “Shame says ‘I am a mistake.’ Responsibility says ‘I made a mistake – and I can do something different.’”

I’m a failure → I failed at something
I’m toxic → I acted in a toxic way
I’m beyond help → I haven’t found the right help yet
Shame → I release what isn’t mine to carry

Open loop behavior: hearing the voice that calls you a failure

12. Session 09 – Decoding the Fire: Your Brain Rewired

Cold open: “Your brain is not a crime scene. It’s a construction site. Trauma rewired it – but you can rewire it again.”

Damaged → adapting
Crazy → triggered
Too much → too aware
Broken → ready to rebuild

Open loop behavior: your body reacting before your mind catches up

13. Session 10 – Letters of Healing: Writing as Recovery

Cold open: “Daniel wrote a letter to his children. To his ex‑wife. To his younger self hiding under the blanket. He didn’t mail them all. But he wrote them. And that act – putting pain into words – changed everything.”

Trauma dumping → finally speaking
Ruminating → trying to solve the unsolvable
Overwriting → revising your own narrative
Burning the letter → releasing what you don’t need to carry

Open loop behavior: the urge to dump everything on someone new

14. Session 11 – The World You Deserve: Building a Trauma‑Informed Future

Cold open: “Daniel didn’t need someone to save him. He needed a world that didn’t punish him for surviving.”

Helpless → informed
Silent → strategic
Alone → part of a movement
Surviving → leading

Open loop behavior: believing the old labels again

Cliffhanger (landing): “This is the end of our formal sessions. But it’s the beginning of your own work. Next session, we lock it in – so you don’t just understand, you become.”

15. Session 12 – You’re Not Who You Thought You Were (Identity Lock‑In)

Cold open: “If you’ve made it this far… you’re not the same person who walked into Session 01. Even if it doesn’t feel dramatic… something has shifted.”

Core concept: “Over the last 11 sessions… you’ve been handed an explanation. Not excuses. Not labels. Explanations. You learned that what you called laziness was exhaustion. Weakness was survival. Broken was adaptation. That matters – because you can’t change something if you think it’s who you are.”

Mirror (with recognition spike): “Think about who you believed you were before you walked in here. Now think about what you understand now. Just notice the difference.”

Lazy → exhausted
Too much → overwhelmed system
Selfish → learning boundaries
Broken → adapted
Failure → still here

Identity lock‑in: “You didn’t just learn something. You changed how you see yourself. And that change doesn’t need to be loud. It just needs to stay.”

Anchor assignment: “Take the glossary card you were given in Session 00. Keep it somewhere you’ll see it. When the old voice comes back – the one that says you’re broken – you look at that card and you say out loud: ‘That was then. I’m not there anymore.’ That’s not pretending. That’s practice.”

Final close: “You’ve already proven you can survive. Now go prove you can live.”
